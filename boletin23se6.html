6.1 General description

Luminescence dating techniques are well established for providing sedimentation ages of deposits on Earth. It is a standard technique with many successful applications. The feasibility of a luminescence-based approach to the dating of soil simulants and sediments analogs to Martian deposits has also been demonstrated. An instrument able to provide such data will be useful in


Figure 29: The IN-TIME project logo.

decision making, pre-screening of samples for sample-return missions, and on-site investigations of regolith or dune time-movement. It will enable complementary data in the geochronology of aeolian, periglacial, and ﬂuvial deposits to understand Mars recent climate history and surface processes; carry out mineral analysis of local geology; and measurement of radiation on the surface of Mars in preparation for human exploration.

Knowledge of mineralogy and chemistry of the Martian surface is of fundamental importance to shape luminescence dating methods because current techniques have been developed for terrestrial material. Remotely sensed data for the planet Mars are readily available for the characterization of the surface and atmosphere. The characterization of such data is primarily of interest to hydrologists, geomorphologists, geologists, and geophysicists, all of them dealing with global scale or at least regional-scale studies.

We are in the preliminary stage of the Project and here we present a panoramic view of the associated work in progress at Universidad Complutense de Madrid, where the Group of Martian Studies carries out its activities [Vázquez et al., 2017].

6.2 Martian radiation environment

Obtaining an accurate estimation of absolute ages of Martian sediments is necessary to understand the geological processes and Mars climate evolution. An eﬀort to improve the geochronology of Mars is carried out in the framework of the IN-TIME European project, in which academia and industry partners (Figure ??) are working together with the aim of increasing our knowledge on the issue. This collaboration is critical, as the aforementioned topic deals with a lot of interdisciplinary areas. One of the Spanish project partners is the University Complutense of Madrid, involving researchers from the Faculty of Computer Science and the Faculty of Physics. Here are presented some of the scientiﬁc aspects of the project in which members of this group and collaborators from the University of Valladolid are directly participating.


Figure 30: IN-TIME participant institutions.

In order to date Mars surface and sub-surface sediments, IN-TIME aims to build a luminescence-based instrument, which is intended to become part of future missions rovers’ payload. In addition, a miniaturized version of the instrument will be developed as an application to be used on Earth in disciplines such as archaeology, geology or risk assessment for emergency events where dosimetry is required. Luminescence dating relies on defects in the crystal lattice of dosimeter minerals, in order to trap energy produced during the interaction between electrons within the crystal. Therefore, an accurate characterization of the background radiation from radionuclide radioactive decay and galactic cosmic rays is really crucial to properly use this technique. Consequently, the deﬁnition of the Mars radiation environment needs to be addressed in the initial part of the project. Some previous studies have dealt with this topic using measurements from earlier missions. On the other hand, a few Martian radiation models have been developed, focusing on the fact that measuring the radiation on the surface of Mars is essential prior to human exploration.
To begin with it, the main sources contributing to radiation at Mars surface and subsurface [Zorzano et al., 2005, Martinez-Frias et al., 2006, Amaral et al., 2007] need to be addressed. For simplicity, we divide them into two groups: external and internal sources. Among the external sources we have, naturally, the solar radiation, the galactic cosmic rays (GCR), and the solar energetic particles (SEP). The solar Mars radiation environment at the planet surface has been already modeled [Vicente-Retortillo, 2017]. Nonetheless, it needs to be complemented taking into account the contributions of the GCR and SEP. Moreover, it is required to calculate the attenuation curve of these contributions from surface down to 3 meters below Mars surface, as this is the drilling capabilities limit planned for the near future missions to Mars by both the American National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) and the European Space Agency (ESA). On the other hand, regarding the internal sources, it is necessary to take into account the composition of Mars surface and subsurface. Again, for this application we will be interested down to 3 meters depth. Of course, it will be essential to properly parameterize the average contribution to the radiation environment (and attenuation) from each of the soil mineral species.


Figure 31: Time evolution of dose rate registered at Mars surface, with indication of hard and soft SEP events. Adapted from [Hassler et al., 2014].

For this radiation environment characterization, it is convenient to consider integrated quantities, such as the dose or the dose equivalent. The available data in order to start building a model mostly correspond to the Mars Science Laboratory Radiation Assessment Detector (MSLRAD), which will provide key information of surface measurements [Matthiä et al., 2017].


Figure 32: Radiation dose rate and pressure at Mars surface. From [Hassler et al., 2014].

Regarding GCR and SEP, it is important to notice that Mars does not have a global magnetic ﬁeld like the Earth. It is thought that Mars actually had once in the past a global magnetic ﬁeld, but billions of years ago its driving mechanism shut down for unknown reasons. Nowadays, there are only leftovers of this older magnetism: magnetised minerals which provide patches of local magnetic ﬁeld. Therefore, the surface of the planet is much more exposed to space radiation than the Earth, due to the lack of a strong global magnetic shield.

Actually, it has been found that the GCR dose rate received at the surface is aﬀected by diﬀerent issues, including the diurnal variations of the atmospheric pressure or the heliospheric structure variability due to the solar activity and rotation. Moreover, the observations show some seasonal variations at Gale crater in Mars. Regarding this fact, it has been reported [Hassler et al., 2014] a decrease in the radiation measured at Mars surface related to the occurrence of soft SEP events, which are actually interplanetary coronal mass ejections that serve as magnetic shields against the GCR (Figure 32).

Finally, it is convenient to look for connections between the atmospheric variables and the radiation environment. In this case, an observational signiﬁcant correlation with atmospheric pressure was found by [Hassler et al., 2014], as shown in Figure 33. All these issues need to be taken into account for a proper characterization and modeling of the Mars radiation environment.

6.3 Martian atmosphere

The growing and unceasing interest in Martian exploration along with the advances in the improvement of the terrestrial meteorological limited area models, has propitiated the ideal scenario in order to obtain the perfect adjustment of these models to the Mars atmosphere.

In this context, an update of the Martian studies is presented in the book “The Atmosphere and Climate of Mars”5 where the Complutense Group has beed deeply involved as in the publications [M. et al., 2009b, M. et al., 2009a, M. et al., 2011, P. et al., 2011].

The use of these models has become an essential tool when evaluating the risks in the stages of entry, descent and landing. Similarly, they allow us to better understand the so complex and interesting atmospheric processes that occur in the Martian atmosphere in conjunction with their eﬀects on the operation of every lander’s instrument [Mateo-Martí et al., 2006, Smith and Lemmon, 1999]. Therefore, it is intended to deﬁne and develop a local numerical model capable of reproducing diﬀerent meteorological processes, characterizing the possible eﬀects of radiation on the Mars surface and atmosphere [N. et al., 2002, R. et al., 2007, S. and F., 2009].

Concerning the Mars atmospheric modelling, it must be remarked the use of diﬀerent models:

Atmospheric Dynamical Model

  • General Circulation Model (GCM): A global numerical model (known as a General Circulation Models - GCM) integrates the primitive equations of atmospheric motion and a range of other physical constraints and ‘forcings’ to yield a simulation of the global atmosphere and the global climate. GCMs have been applied to a variety of planetary atmospheres since the 1960s [Leovy and Mintz, 1969], not long after their introduction into terrestrial atmospheric science.

On the other hand, the limited area (or meso/micro-scale) models have only recently been used for planetary atmospheres. They have been almost exclusively applied to Mars in order to interpret landed spacecraft data and to examine meteorological systems on substantially sub-global scales [Toigo and Richardson, 2002, Tyler et al., 2002], though more recently they have also been applied to look at cloud formation on Titan. These models allow simulation of dynamics on scales down to a few meters (so-called Large Eddy Simulation or LES modeling) in order to explicitly simulate the boundary layer.

However, the restricted nature of the computational grids of these models has limited them to non-global domain extents. As such, the boundary conditions of these limited area models must either be forced from an archive of global model results, or set to be periodic in idealized simulations.

  • Mars Regional Atmospheric Modeling System (MRAMS): This model simulates the circulations within the Martian atmosphere at regional and local scales. It has a non-hydrostatic dynamics, which leads to the solution of the vertical momentum equation. Consequently, these kind of models are successfully used for horizontal scales of the order of 100 m, resolving small-scale meso-scale circulations such as cumulus convection and sea-breeze circulations.

However, the obtained results are not completely reliable due to the grid eﬀects and complex micro-scale dynamics (convective vortices or micro-scale turbulences). Therefore, it is necessary the use of another kind of simulation, such as the Large Eddy Simulations (LES), to complement it.

  • Laboratoire de Météorologie Dynamique (LMD) mesoscale model: Following the scientiﬁc activities of Mars Express, the LMD Martian Meso-scale Model was developed in 2007 as an interface between an adapted version of the terrestrial WRF model and the physical parameterizations developed for the LMD Global Climate Model. Since its inception, the model has been applied to various topics of interest to understand the variability of the Martian atmosphere at spatial scales smaller than those resolved by GCMs [N. et al., 2002, Ordonez-Etxeberria et al., 2019, S. and F., 2009, Spiga et al., 2013]: Slope ﬂows and their thermal impact, boundary-layer turbulence and dust devils (with the Large-Eddy Simulations), gravity waves (notably their impact on the formation of CO2 clouds), polar meteorology and katabatic jumps, dust-induced convection in “rocket dust storms” or ice-induced convection in “turbulent snowstorms”.

Simulations with the LMD Martian Meso-scale Model helped to interpret the results obtained from space missions in the last decade, contributing to an international community eﬀort with Martian Meso-scale Models developed and used in other teams. The approach developed at LMD for Martian meso-scale modeling has also been recently transposed to Venus. The InSight mission to Mars opens new perspectives for meso-scale and micro-scale atmospheric science, where the use of small-scale simulations has proven useful to prepare the mission’s observations and will be instrumental to interpret the unprecedented signals caught by the InSight instruments.

  • Large Eddy Simulation (LES) model: This micro-scale model is able to resolve the daytime convective motions which can create strong updrafts and downdrafts. Is has a non-hydrostatic ﬂuid dynamical core with an horizontal resolution of 10 to 50km so as to resolve convective cells. Moreover, scales below 10 km and 1 km, are dominated by turbulent gusts and dust devils, two distinct convective boundary layer processes likely to lift dust from the surface. Therefore, convective vortices have an enhanced ability to both lift and inject dust in the atmosphere [Lemmon et al., 2015, N. et al., 2002, Ordonez-Etxeberria et al., 2019].
  • planetWRF model: This 3D multi-scale model is based on the already existing model with nesting capabilities, the Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) model. It eliminates the distinction between global and meso-scale models. In this way, the model simulations can extent from global-to-local scales, i.e., from hundred meters to 10.000 kms, which can be important for problems such as the onset and expansion of Martian dust storms or dunes formation and migration. It has a generalized map-projection, which allows the model domain to be centered and shaped in lots of diﬀerent ways [R. et al., 2007, Vicente-Retortillo et al., 2017].

Radiative Transfer Model
From this model, an interpretation of remote sensing observation (i.e., satellite, ground-based measurements, etc.) can be made. It must be taken into account the key-role played by dust, which can modify considerable the atmosphere and superﬁcial temperature. Moreover, airborne dust can lead to a strong dynamic due to sudden change in the vertical temperature [B. and D., 2008, C. et al., 2013, De A. et al., 2004, D.-B. et al., 2016].

  • Monte Carlo model:
    This model simulates 3D solar radiative transfer through the atmosphere. The code can calculate radiative ﬂuxes and radiances (for any view direction) at the top or bottom of the domain, and radiative heating rates throughout the domain.

Photo-chemical Model
These are large-scale air quality models that simulate the changes of pollutant concentrations in the atmosphere. They are applied at multiple spatial scales (global, regional and local scales).

6.4 Computing

From what has been explained in the previous sections, the IN-TIME project demands a great amount of computing power for data processing and model execution. This will be provided by the cloud paradigm, which allows access to dynamic, elastic and on demand computing resources.

Nowadays the cloud is an essential tool for Space missions data processing. The IN-TIME team has experience providing solutions in related projects and missions. Some examples follow:

  • Eﬃcient Onboard Actualization of an Instrumental Cyclogram for the Mars MetNet Mission [Romero et al., 2011, Vázquez-Poletti et al., 2012].
  • Diﬀerent parallelisation strategies for the Cherenkov Telescope Array’s real-time-analysis software [Rodríguez-Vázquez et al., 2017].
  • Modeling and Simulation of the Atmospheric Dust Dynamic [Vázquez et al., 2018].
  • Mars Express MARSIS ionogram eﬃcient processing in real time [V.-P. and L., 2018].
  • Eﬃcient processing of Venus Express VMC images [Vázquez-Poletti et al., 2019].
