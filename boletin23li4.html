Martian radiation environment

Obtaining an accurate estimation of absolute ages of Martian sediments is necessary to understand the geological processes and Mars climate evolution. An eﬀort to improve the geochronology of Mars is carried out in the framework of the IN-TIME European project, in which academia and industry partners (Figure ??) are working together with the aim of increasing our knowledge on the issue. This collaboration is critical, as the aforementioned topic deals with a lot of interdisciplinary areas. One of the Spanish project partners is the University Complutense of Madrid, involving researchers from the Faculty of Computer Science and the Faculty of Physics. Here are presented some of the scientiﬁc aspects of the project in which members of this group and collaborators from the University of Valladolid are directly participating.


Figure 30: IN-TIME participant institutions.

In order to date Mars surface and sub-surface sediments, IN-TIME aims to build a luminescence-based instrument, which is intended to become part of future missions rovers’ payload. In addition, a miniaturized version of the instrument will be developed as an application to be used on Earth in disciplines such as archaeology, geology or risk assessment for emergency events where dosimetry is required. Luminescence dating relies on defects in the crystal lattice of dosimeter minerals, in order to trap energy produced during the interaction between electrons within the crystal. Therefore, an accurate characterization of the background radiation from radionuclide radioactive decay and galactic cosmic rays is really crucial to properly use this technique. Consequently, the deﬁnition of the Mars radiation environment needs to be addressed in the initial part of the project. Some previous studies have dealt with this topic using measurements from earlier missions. On the other hand, a few Martian radiation models have been developed, focusing on the fact that measuring the radiation on the surface of Mars is essential prior to human exploration.
To begin with it, the main sources contributing to radiation at Mars surface and subsurface [?, ?, ?] need to be addressed. For simplicity, we divide them into two groups: external and internal sources. Among the external sources we have, naturally, the solar radiation, the galactic cosmic rays (GCR), and the solar energetic particles (SEP). The solar Mars radiation environment at the planet surface has been already modeled [?]. Nonetheless, it needs to be complemented taking into account the contributions of the GCR and SEP. Moreover, it is required to calculate the attenuation curve of these contributions from surface down to 3 meters below Mars surface, as this is the drilling capabilities limit planned for the near future missions to Mars by both the American National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) and the European Space Agency (ESA). On the other hand, regarding the internal sources, it is necessary to take into account the composition of Mars surface and subsurface. Again, for this application we will be interested down to 3 meters depth. Of course, it will be essential to properly parameterize the average contribution to the radiation environment (and attenuation) from each of the soil mineral species.

For this radiation environment characterization, it is convenient to consider integrated quantities, such as the dose or the dose equivalent. The available data in order to start building a model mostly correspond to the Mars Science Laboratory Radiation Assessment Detector (MSLRAD), which will provide key information of surface measurements [?].


Figure 32: Radiation dose rate and pressure at Mars surface. From [?].

Regarding GCR and SEP, it is important to notice that Mars does not have a global magnetic ﬁeld like the Earth. It is thought that Mars actually had once in the past a global magnetic ﬁeld, but billions of years ago its driving mechanism shut down for unknown reasons. Nowadays, there are only leftovers of this older magnetism: magnetised minerals which provide patches of local magnetic ﬁeld. Therefore, the surface of the planet is much more exposed to space radiation than the Earth, due to the lack of a strong global magnetic shield.

Actually, it has been found that the GCR dose rate received at the surface is aﬀected by diﬀerent issues, including the diurnal variations of the atmospheric pressure or the heliospheric structure variability due to the solar activity and rotation. Moreover, the observations show some seasonal variations at Gale crater in Mars. Regarding this fact, it has been reported [?] a decrease in the radiation measured at Mars surface related to the occurrence of soft SEP events, which are actually interplanetary coronal mass ejections that serve as magnetic shields against the GCR (Figure ??).

Finally, it is convenient to look for connections between the atmospheric variables and the radiation environment. In this case, an observational signiﬁcant correlation with atmospheric pressure was found by [?], as shown in Figure ??. All these issues need to be taken into account for a proper characterization and modeling of the Mars radiation environment.
