Martian atmosphere

The growing and unceasing interest in Martian exploration along with the advances in the improvement of the terrestrial meteorological limited area models, has propitiated the ideal scenario in order to obtain the perfect adjustment of these models to the Mars atmosphere.

In this context, an update of the Martian studies is presented in the book “The Atmosphere and Climate of Mars”5 where the Complutense Group has beed deeply involved as in the publications [?, ?, ?, ?].

The use of these models has become an essential tool when evaluating the risks in the stages of entry, descent and landing. Similarly, they allow us to better understand the so complex and interesting atmospheric processes that occur in the Martian atmosphere in conjunction with their eﬀects on the operation of every lander’s instrument [?, ?]. Therefore, it is intended to deﬁne and develop a local numerical model capable of reproducing diﬀerent meteorological processes, characterizing the possible eﬀects of radiation on the Mars surface and atmosphere [?, ?, ?].

Concerning the Mars atmospheric modelling, it must be remarked the use of diﬀerent models:

Atmospheric Dynamical Model

  • General Circulation Model (GCM): A global numerical model (known as a General Circulation Models - GCM) integrates the primitive equations of atmospheric motion and a range of other physical constraints and ‘forcings’ to yield a simulation of the global atmosphere and the global climate. GCMs have been applied to a variety of planetary atmospheres since the 1960s [?], not long after their introduction into terrestrial atmospheric science.

On the other hand, the limited area (or meso/micro-scale) models have only recently been used for planetary atmospheres. They have been almost exclusively applied to Mars in order to interpret landed spacecraft data and to examine meteorological systems on substantially sub-global scales [?, ?], though more recently they have also been applied to look at cloud formation on Titan. These models allow simulation of dynamics on scales down to a few meters (so-called Large Eddy Simulation or LES modeling) in order to explicitly simulate the boundary layer.

However, the restricted nature of the computational grids of these models has limited them to non-global domain extents. As such, the boundary conditions of these limited area models must either be forced from an archive of global model results, or set to be periodic in idealized simulations.

  • Mars Regional Atmospheric Modeling System (MRAMS): This model simulates the circulations within the Martian atmosphere at regional and local scales. It has a non-hydrostatic dynamics, which leads to the solution of the vertical momentum equation. Consequently, these kind of models are successfully used for horizontal scales of the order of 100 m, resolving small-scale meso-scale circulations such as cumulus convection and sea-breeze circulations.

However, the obtained results are not completely reliable due to the grid eﬀects and complex micro-scale dynamics (convective vortices or micro-scale turbulences). Therefore, it is necessary the use of another kind of simulation, such as the Large Eddy Simulations (LES), to complement it.

  • Laboratoire de Météorologie Dynamique (LMD) mesoscale model: Following the scientiﬁc activities of Mars Express, the LMD Martian Meso-scale Model was developed in 2007 as an interface between an adapted version of the terrestrial WRF model and the physical parameterizations developed for the LMD Global Climate Model. Since its inception, the model has been applied to various topics of interest to understand the variability of the Martian atmosphere at spatial scales smaller than those resolved by GCMs [?, ?, ?, ?]: Slope ﬂows and their thermal impact, boundary-layer turbulence and dust devils (with the Large-Eddy Simulations), gravity waves (notably their impact on the formation of CO2 clouds), polar meteorology and katabatic jumps, dust-induced convection in “rocket dust storms” or ice-induced convection in “turbulent snowstorms”.

Simulations with the LMD Martian Meso-scale Model helped to interpret the results obtained from space missions in the last decade, contributing to an international community eﬀort with Martian Meso-scale Models developed and used in other teams. The approach developed at LMD for Martian meso-scale modeling has also been recently transposed to Venus. The InSight mission to Mars opens new perspectives for meso-scale and micro-scale atmospheric science, where the use of small-scale simulations has proven useful to prepare the mission’s observations and will be instrumental to interpret the unprecedented signals caught by the InSight instruments.

  • Large Eddy Simulation (LES) model: This micro-scale model is able to resolve the daytime convective motions which can create strong updrafts and downdrafts. Is has a non-hydrostatic ﬂuid dynamical core with an horizontal resolution of 10 to 50km so as to resolve convective cells. Moreover, scales below 10 km and 1 km, are dominated by turbulent gusts and dust devils, two distinct convective boundary layer processes likely to lift dust from the surface. Therefore, convective vortices have an enhanced ability to both lift and inject dust in the atmosphere [?, ?, ?].
  • planetWRF model: This 3D multi-scale model is based on the already existing model with nesting capabilities, the Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) model. It eliminates the distinction between global and meso-scale models. In this way, the model simulations can extent from global-to-local scales, i.e., from hundred meters to 10.000 kms, which can be important for problems such as the onset and expansion of Martian dust storms or dunes formation and migration. It has a generalized map-projection, which allows the model domain to be centered and shaped in lots of diﬀerent ways [?, ?].

Radiative Transfer Model
From this model, an interpretation of remote sensing observation (i.e., satellite, ground-based measurements, etc.) can be made. It must be taken into account the key-role played by dust, which can modify considerable the atmosphere and superﬁcial temperature. Moreover, airborne dust can lead to a strong dynamic due to sudden change in the vertical temperature [?, ?, ?, ?].

  • Monte Carlo model:
    This model simulates 3D solar radiative transfer through the atmosphere. The code can calculate radiative ﬂuxes and radiances (for any view direction) at the top or bottom of the domain, and radiative heating rates throughout the domain.

Photo-chemical Model
These are large-scale air quality models that simulate the changes of pollutant concentrations in the atmosphere. They are applied at multiple spatial scales (global, regional and local scales).
